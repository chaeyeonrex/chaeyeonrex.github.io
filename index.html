The Architecture of Choice: Reclaiming Rationality through Curved Economics.

Research Philosophy

Economic behavior often deviates from static optimization. This is not due to irrationality, but because movement itself is structurally constrained. My research reconceptualizes rationality as 'directional accessibility under structural friction.'

Economic agents do not instantaneously pick from a set of choices; they navigate an 'invisible terrain' where costs and constraints exist. Behaviors like delay, status quo bias, and asymmetric responses may not be psychological biases, but rather the optimized outcomes of a rational agent facing a curved landscape.

This perspective provides a new language to unify microeconomics, macroeconomics, behavioral economics, and game theory, extending economics from a 'theory of choice' to a 'geometry of possible movement.'

Core Concepts

Curved Economics

Reconstructs the economic decision-making space not as a traditional Euclidean space ('flat space'), but as a Riemannian manifold ('curved space') where the path itself has costs and constraints. It explains economic behavior as a process of searching for the most efficient path, rather than instantly moving to the optimal choice.

Reachability Equilibrium (XRE)

A new equilibrium concept where a strategy becomes a valid option only when it is cost-effectively 'reachable.' This maintains the conventional assumption of rationality while explaining various behavioral phenomena (e.g., delay, asymmetry) as rational outcomes of structural constraints.

Structural Friction

The invisible costs that impede behavioral change. These include an 'activation threshold' that requires a shock of a certain intensity to trigger a response, 'directional asymmetry' where the costs of moving forward and backward differ, and 'path dependence' where past trajectories affect current friction.

Classifying Homo Economicus' Behavior: Rationality Reclaimed through Reachability Equilibrium

July, 2025 (Preliminary)

Abstract

This paper introduces the Reachability Equilibrium (XRE) framework, where deviations from static optimization arise from structural constraints on adjustment rather than from irrationality. We model the latent geometry of the adjustment space using optimal control methods. Applying XRE to NASDAQ data from the dot-com era (1995-2005), we identify key nonlinear dynamics, including activation thresholds, VIX-amplified asymmetry, and path dependence. Extending this approach to online platforms, we analyze Google Trends and pageview data related to YouTube policy and find the same structural dynamics. XRE provides a unified framework to reinterpret these anomalies as rational responses to an invisible terrain.

Key Contributions

  • Theoretical Framework: Proposes **Reachability Equilibrium (XRE)**, where rationality is redefined as optimal navigation across a 'curved' decision space with structural frictions.
  • Reinterpretation of Anomalies: Reframes behavioral patterns like inertia and loss aversion not as psychological biases, but as emergent properties of the adjustment terrain's geometry (e.g., activation thresholds, directional asymmetry).
  • Empirical Validation (Finance): Validates the framework using NASDAQ data from the dot-com era, identifying VIX-amplified asymmetry and macro-conditional path dependence.
  • Empirical Validation (Platforms): Demonstrates the framework's generalizability by finding the same structural dynamics in online behavior following YouTube policy changes.

Notes on Curved Economics

March - May, 2025 (Weekly Drafts)

Overview

A collection of research notes written over 11 weeks, documenting the theoretical development of 'Curved Economics.' These notes detail the process of geometrically reinterpreting economic decision spaces and further modeling complex phenomena like path dependence and identity fragmentation through abstract algebra.

Key Concepts Explored

  • Riemannian Manifolds: Models the decision-making space as a 'curved space' to analyze the costs of the path itself.
  • Quaternions: Uses quaternions, where the order of multiplication matters, to mathematically represent path dependence, where the sequence of events affects the outcome.
  • Octonions: Employs non-associative octonions to explain strategic collapse and identity fragmentation under extreme cognitive load.
  • Ontological Economics: Interprets 'inaction' as a rational and ethical choice for maintaining ontological stability.

Observed Matching under Projection Constraints

July, 2025 (Working Notes)

Overview

This work proposes that informational frictions can arise from the architecture of perception itself. It develops a theory where agents make decisions based on a 'projection' of the world, not its full structure. This can lead to situations where fundamentally different economic states appear identical to an observer, challenging standard identification assumptions in matching and trade models.

Key Implications

  • Perceptual Equivalence: Structurally different types or states can become observationally indistinguishable when viewed through a projection.
  • Observer-Relative Continuity: A path of change can seem smooth to an observer even if it contains abrupt structural breaks, potentially delaying belief updates.
  • Matching on Projections: Matching mechanisms may operate on "perceived types" rather than true identities, leading to coarse outcomes where distinct types are treated the same.
  • Use Case in Trade: The framework explains how two countries with different fundamentals might exhibit identical trade flows, as their structural differences are compressed by the observer's "lens."
